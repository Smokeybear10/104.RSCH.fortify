About

Fortify the Ordnance

A working archive of every weapon proposal and research dollar that passed through the U.S. Board of Ordnance & Fortification, 1888 to 1920.

What this is

Between 1888 and 1920, a small federal committee called the Board of Ordnance & Fortification decided which new weapons technologies the U.S. Army should fund, test, and adopt. Inventors, both private citizens and government engineers, submitted ideas. The Board reviewed them, awarded grants, ran tests, and either greenlit the design for service or filed it away.

For thirty-two years the Board sat at the center of American military innovation, vetting everything from disappearing gun carriages and dirigible torpedoes to the Wright brothers' aerodrome and the Luger automatic pistol. Then in 1920 it was quietly dissolved, and the records went into archive boxes.

This site pulls those records back out and makes them readable. The dashboard lets you filter, drill into, and export every proposal, every dollar, and every reviewed technology.

Why it matters

The BOF's three decades cover one of the most violent technological transitions in military history. The Army that opened the Board's docket in 1888 was still fighting with black-powder rifles and horse-drawn artillery. The Army that closed it in 1920 had survived a war fought with machine guns, dreadnoughts, poison gas, submarines, and aircraft.

The Board sat squarely on that fault line. Reading its records is reading bureaucracy figuring out, in real time, what it didn't yet know how to think about. A 1898 entry says "Not recommended" on a proposal for armored cars. A 1907 entry says "Allotment" on the Wright brothers' airplane. The pattern of yeses and nos is the actual texture of how a state decides what to bet on.

What we built

The dashboard has eight sections. Four are interactive data views, pulled from the original Board reports and structured into filterable, searchable tables and charts. Four are researcher analyses, each one a different angle on the same archive.

The four data views cover the proposals submitted to the Board, the broader U.S. military budget those proposals lived inside of, a hand-classified review timeline of every technology the Board considered, and the BOF's own spending down to individual line items.

The four researcher analyses sit alongside as separate sections. Each researcher pulled a different thread from the source material.

The data

Everything on the dashboard comes from primary sources: the Board's annual reports to Congress, fiscal year appropriations acts, and the original "subjects considered" tables. The records were transcribed into spreadsheets, then cleaned, normalized, and structured into the CSV files that power the live views.

The classification system, what counts as Artillery vs Small Arms, what's a government inventor vs a private one, what the Board's actual decision was in ambiguous cases, is documented in the project's classifier and applied consistently across every record.

Sources

Raw research files (Excel spreadsheets, scanned PDFs of original reports) live in a private companion repository. Only cleaned, derived data ships with the public dashboard.

Yunha's canonical visual site at paull0318.github.io/BOF-Visuals-20260511 extends coverage back to 1888 and is the authoritative external reference.

Every number on the dashboard was audited against its source CSV. Known gaps, transcription quirks, and the residual data limitations are documented on the data quality page.
