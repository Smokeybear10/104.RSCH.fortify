What is Fortify the Ordnance?

It's a research dashboard that takes 30+ years of forgotten federal paperwork and turns it into something you can actually click through. This page exists so you don't have to guess what each tab does or why we built it.

TL;DR The 60-second version

What it is
An interactive archive of every weapon proposal and research dollar that passed through the U.S. Board of Ordnance & Fortification between 1888 and 1920.
Why it exists
The source records — Excel spreadsheets, scanned PDFs, hand-transcribed timelines — are unreadable as raw files. The dashboard is the readable version.
Who built it
Four researchers: Thomas Ou (pipeline + dashboard), Yunha (financial × tech), Paul B (technology review log), Tanisha (type prevalence).
How to use it
Pick a tab at the top, narrow with filters, click into chart bars or table rows for detail, export CSVs of what you're looking at.

01 What was the Board of Ordnance & Fortification?

The U.S. Board of Ordnance & Fortification (BOF) was a federal body created in 1888 to decide which new weapons technologies the Army should adopt. Inventors — both private citizens and government engineers — would submit ideas. The Board would review them, run tests, and either reject the proposal or award money ("an allotment") to develop it further.

Think of it as a 19th-century version of DARPA: a small committee with discretionary funding that decided which gadgets got prototyped. The Board operated from 1888 to 1920, covering the technology gap between the Civil War's muzzle-loaders and World War I's machine-gun-and-aircraft warfare.

Context During the BOF's lifetime, the Army went from black-powder rifles to smokeless ammunition, from sail-and-coal navy support to dreadnoughts, and from horse-drawn artillery to motorized transport. The Board was at the center of approving (or rejecting) the inventions that made those transitions possible.

02 How the dashboard is laid out

The page has two halves:

  1. The top half = four interactive views (tabs labeled Proposals · Budget · Timeline · Spending). Each one filters and visualizes one slice of the data.
  2. The bottom half = the deliverables section — four numbered bars (01–04) with thumbnail cards underneath. Each one links to a chart or analysis a specific researcher built.

The header at the very top is just identity and stats — name of the project, record counts, team list, GitHub link. Nothing to interact with.

03 The four tabs (top half)

Each tab shares the same pattern: filter bar → two charts → records table → export CSV.

PROPOSALS 1897–1908 · 1,691 records

Every weapon proposal submitted to the BOF during the years it kept detailed "subjects considered" reports (1897–1908). Each record is one inventor pitching one idea — with the Board's response.

Use this when: you want to know what people were trying to invent, who submitted it, and whether the Board said yes / no / maybe.

BUDGET 1865–1920 · 110 appropriations

The total U.S. military budget — Army and Navy combined — across 55 years. This is the big-picture spending context that the BOF operated inside of. It is not BOF-specific.

Use this when: you want to know the macro scale — how much Congress was giving the military each year, and how that grew through Reconstruction, the Spanish-American War, and the lead-up to WWI.

TIMELINE 1888–1916 · 202 technologies · Paul B's deliverable

A sticky-column matrix where each row is one technology (e.g. "12-inch gun barbette") and each column is one of the Board's reporting periods. The cells are color-coded by outcome — accepted, rejected, under test, referred.

Use this when: you want to trace the full life of one technology — when it first showed up, how the Board responded over time, and what eventually happened.

SPENDING 1888–1920 · 1,382 allotments · Thomas's deliverable

The actual money trail. Two layers stacked: the yearly lump sum Congress gave the BOF (the "appropriation"), and every individual research grant the BOF then handed out (the "allotments"). Includes both active grants and ones that got revoked later.

Use this when: you want to follow specific dollars — like "what did the BOF actually spend $1,000 on in 1891?"

04 Why are there two money tabs?

Because they answer two different scales of question:

BUDGET
Macro. How big was the entire U.S. military budget each year? Hundreds of millions / billions of dollars. Army + Navy combined. Predates and outlives the BOF.
SPENDING
Micro. Of the slice that went to the BOF specifically, what did they spend it on — line item by line item? Hundreds to thousands of dollars per grant.

Right now these two tabs aren't linked in the UI. The Budget view shows the forest; the Spending view shows the trees. They share the same time axis (~1888–1920) and that's where you'd cross-reference manually.

06 How to read the charts

Time-series charts submissions per year · budget over time · spending over time

  • X axis = year (always).
  • Y axis = count or dollar amount.
  • Stacked colors = subcategories — status (Approved / Investigating / Rejected / Other) or branch (Army / Navy).
  • Dotted vertical lines at 1898 and 1917 = Spanish-American War + U.S. enters WWI, for historical context.
  • Hover = exact numbers per year per category.
  • Click a bar in Proposals → drills the filter to that year.

Donut charts cluster mix · branch mix · active vs revoked

  • Each slice = one category, sized by its share.
  • The big number in the middle = the total (records, dollars, etc.) for whatever filter you have applied.
  • Click a slice = filter the rest of the dashboard to just that category.

Records tables below the charts in every view

  • Each row is one underlying record — one proposal, one budget line, one allotment.
  • Click any column header to sort by it.
  • Click any row to open a detail modal with the full text (Board's action, reasoning, sources).
  • Paginated at 50 rows per page.

The technology review matrix timeline tab only

  • Each row = one specific technology (e.g. "3-inch dynamite gun").
  • Each column = one BOF reporting period.
  • Each colored bar = the Board's action that period. Green = accepted, red = rejected, orange = under test, gray = referred / filed.
  • Hover any bar = the original action text from the source records.
  • A small number badge means the Board acted on it multiple times in that period.

07 Vocab — things you'll see a lot

Proposal / Subject
One weapon idea submitted to the Board. From the "Subjects Considered" annual reports.
Appropriation
A lump sum Congress hands over each year. The BOF got one yearly appropriation it could then split up.
Allotment
A specific grant the BOF awarded out of its appropriation — usually a few hundred to a few thousand dollars for a specific experiment or prototype.
Revoked allotment
A grant that was awarded but later pulled back — maybe the inventor failed to deliver, maybe the project was cancelled.
Cluster (technology cluster)
A coarse category we use to group proposals: Artillery, Explosives, Small Arms, Armor & Protection, Fortification & Engineering, Communications & Observation, Logistics & Support, Other.
Status
The Board's outcome on a proposal. We bucket the raw text into four: Approved, Investigating, Rejected, Other.
Proposer type
Whether the submission came from a government employee (Army officer, Navy engineer) or a private inventor / civilian / company.
Nominal vs 2025-adjusted
"Nominal" = dollar amount as printed in the original document. "2025-adjusted" = inflation-corrected to modern dollars. Toggleable on the Budget tab.

08 Common questions

Is the dashboard the full archive?
No. The dashboard is the cleaned, structured slice of the archive that we built pipelines for. The raw source files (scanned PDFs, Excel spreadsheets, hand-written notes) live in a separate private GitHub repo and are not on the dashboard.
Why do some years have no data?
The BOF didn't publish complete reports every single year. Some years are partial, some are entirely missing from the source records — usually 1890s gaps before reporting became standardized.
Why does the Proposals tab stop at 1908 but Spending goes to 1920?
Different source datasets. The detailed "subjects considered" reports (each proposal with its full action text) only run 1897–1908. The financial appropriations data goes the full BOF lifespan, 1888–1920.
Can I download the data?
Yes. Every view has an EXPORT CSV button that downloads the currently-filtered slice as a CSV file you can open in Excel or pandas. The full cleaned outputs also live in output/*.csv in the public GitHub repo.
Why are the deliverable cards mostly static HTML when the tabs are interactive?
The cards link to standalone Plotly HTML files generated by each researcher's Python script. They're self-contained — open in a new browser tab — so they can be shared on their own without the whole dashboard. The interactive tabs at the top use a different rendering path (live data from CSV).
What's the difference between an "appropriation" and an "allotment" — they sound the same?
Appropriation = Congress → BOF (yearly lump sum). Allotment = BOF → specific project (individual grant). The BOF received one appropriation per year and then divided it into many allotments. Both flows are visible in the Spending tab — the line shows the appropriation, the bars show the allotments stacked.
Who is this for?
Originally built for a course research assignment, but useful to anyone studying U.S. military procurement history, late-19th-century invention culture, or how federal agencies allocate R&D dollars over time.

09 Running it locally

If you cloned the repo and want to see it on your machine:

  1. Set up Python deps: pip install -r requirements.txt
  2. Pull the data: ./bootstrap-data.sh (needs gh auth)
  3. Boot the static server: ./serve.sh
  4. Open http://localhost:2104

Port is fixed at 2104. serve.sh kills anything else on that port first. No build step — it's static HTML + JS reading from CSVs.
